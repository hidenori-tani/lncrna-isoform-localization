# Subcellular localization of human long non-coding RNAs is an isoform-resolved property masked by gene-level analysis

## Abstract

The function of a long non-coding RNA (lncRNA) is tightly coupled to its subcellular localization, yet localization is almost always annotated at the **gene** level. Because most lncRNA genes produce multiple isoforms and undergo tissue-specific isoform switching, I ask whether localization is in fact an **isoform-resolved** property that gene-level analysis misrepresents. Reusing tissue-specific dominant-isoform switch calls for 268 lncRNA genes (GTEx long-read), I assigned each isoform a cytoplasmic–nuclear relative concentration index (CN-RCI) measured from ENCODE subcellular fractionation RNA-seq across 15 human cell lines (1,262 isoforms localized). For 163 genes both tissue-dominant isoforms were localized. A substantial, noise-robust fraction—55% overall, 26–38% under stringent confidence filtering—express isoforms occupying **opposite** compartments. Because gene-level CN-RCI is a log-ratio of summed isoform abundances, it sits in the neutral zone and **masks** this split in 28% of genes (19% even under an abundance-aware definition). Tissue switching does not place isoforms in different compartments more than a dominance-matched permutation null (observed = expected = 33%; p = 1.0)—it is compartment-agnostic—and are largely independent of Alu content (p = 0.93 overall; Alu contributes only among the minority of Alu-bearing pairs). An independent total RNA-seq protocol reproduced the isoform localization (Spearman ρ = 0.73; 80% compartment-sign agreement), arguing against a quantification artefact. The robust conclusion is methodological: lncRNA localization is encoded at isoform resolution, and gene-level annotations systematically misrepresent it; isoform-resolved localization should be the default unit of analysis.

**Keywords:** long non-coding RNA; subcellular localization; isoform; alternative transcription; subcellular fractionation; CN-RCI

## 1. Introduction

Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) are functionally diverse, and the single most informative predictor of a lncRNA's mechanism is its steady-state subcellular localization [1,2]. Nuclear-retained lncRNAs scaffold ribonucleoprotein bodies, guide chromatin-modifying complexes, and regulate transcription in *cis*; cytoplasmic lncRNAs act as competing endogenous RNAs, modulate mRNA stability and translation, and influence signalling cascades [1,11]. Resources such as LncATLAS have made gene-level nuclear/cytoplasmic ratios broadly available, and these values are now used routinely as functional priors and as features in predictors of lncRNA function [2,3].

Two facts sit uneasily with this gene-level convention. First, the great majority of lncRNA loci are multi-exonic and multi-isoform, with alternative transcription start sites, splicing and termination generating transcripts that can differ substantially in sequence content, and single-molecule imaging shows that localization can vary among transcripts of the same locus [5]. Second, tissue-specific isoform switching—where the dominant isoform of a gene changes between tissues—is pervasive and is largely invisible to gene-level differential-expression analysis [6]. If localization is determined by isoform-level sequence features, then a gene-level localization value aggregates over isoforms (it is a log-ratio of summed isoform abundances) and may not represent any single transcript, and the effective localization of a gene could vary between tissues simply because a different isoform predominates.

Here I test, directly and with measured data, whether subcellular localization is an isoform-resolved property and whether gene-level analysis misrepresents it. I deliberately separate three questions: (i) **descriptive**—do isoforms of the same lncRNA gene occupy different compartments? (ii) **switching**—does tissue-specific isoform switching cross the nuclear/cytoplasmic boundary more than expected by chance? and (iii) **mechanistic**—is any such compartment difference explained by Alu content, a known driver of nuclear retention? I report the answers transparently, including two informative negatives, and arrive at a robust methodological conclusion.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Tissue-specific isoform switch calls (reused)
Dominant-isoform switch calls were reused from a companion analysis of GTEx long-read transcriptomes [7] (FLAIR isoform models; GENCODE v26 lncRNA annotation [10]; Tani, submitted). Briefly, in that analysis per-tissue isoform-usage fractions were computed for each lncRNA across nine tissues; the dominant isoform per tissue was the most-used isoform; and a gene was called a switch when its dominant isoform differed between tissues with a significant change in isoform-usage composition (label-permutation test, B = 10,000, Benjamini–Hochberg corrected). For each lncRNA gene and each pair of tissues, the calls specify the dominant isoform in each tissue (`dom_isoform_A`, `dom_isoform_B`). The input comprised 2,453 switch events across 268 genes (591 distinct dominant isoforms). No switch calls were recomputed here.

### 2.2 Isoform-level subcellular localization (measured)
Subcellular fractionation RNA-seq experiments (nucleus and cytosol fractions) for *Homo sapiens* were retrieved from the ENCODE portal [9]. To avoid mixing quantification tools, only RSEM [8] transcript-quantification files (GENCODE V29) were used; these were available for both fractions in all 15 cell lines (A549, GM12878, H1, HT1080, HeLa-S3, HepG2, IMR-90, K562, MCF-7, NCI-H460, SK-MEL-5, SK-N-DZ, SK-N-SH, HUVEC, keratinocyte). For each isoform in each cell line, the cytoplasmic–nuclear relative concentration index (CN-RCI), as defined for LncATLAS [3], was computed as CN-RCI = log2(TPM_cytosol / TPM_nucleus). When multiple replicate quantification files existed for a cell line/fraction their TPMs were averaged. A small pseudocount (0.01) was substituted only when a fraction's TPM was exactly zero (to avoid division by zero); non-zero values therefore use exact ratios, preserving the compartment sign on which the analysis depends. A transcript was scored only if at least one fraction reached TPM ≥ 1.0; this floor is conservative and the central finding is stable across floors (the fraction of genes with isoforms in both compartments was 57%, 55% and 50% at TPM ≥ 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0). ENCODE V29 transcript identifiers were bridged to the GENCODE v26 switch calls by stable-ID matching (version stripped); 410 of the 591 distinct dominant isoforms (69.4%) were recovered in the V29 fractionation data. A per-isoform consensus localization was taken as the median CN-RCI across cell lines, with a sign-agreement score (fraction of cell lines agreeing on compartment) retained as a confidence measure. This yielded localization for 1,262 isoforms (914 in ≥2 cell lines), covering 232 of the 268 switch genes.

### 2.3 Compartment classification and permutation null
For each switch event with both dominant isoforms localized (1,735 events, 163 genes), the localization change was ΔRCI = RCI(dom_B) − RCI(dom_A). An event was a **compartment switch** when the two dominant isoforms had opposite RCI signs (opposite compartments) and |ΔRCI| ≥ θ; θ = 1.0 was primary, with θ ∈ {0.5, 1.5, 2.0} for sensitivity. A gene was a compartment-switcher if any of its events qualified. To test whether switching crosses compartments more than expected, a permutation null was built that, for each gene, replaces its tissue-dominant pairs with random pairs drawn from that gene's **dominant** isoforms (those dominant in ≥1 tissue—well-expressed, avoiding minor/pre-mRNA-level transcripts), preserving the per-gene number of switch events, and recomputes the gene-level compartment-switcher fraction (the null reproduces the observed statistic; B = 10,000). For comparison, a looser null drawing from all localized isoforms of the gene was also computed.

### 2.4 Gene-level blind-spot quantification
Gene-level localization was computed as it would be by a gene-level pipeline (e.g. LncATLAS-style [3]): per cell line and fraction, isoform TPMs were summed per gene, CN-RCI = log2(ΣTPM_cytosol / ΣTPM_nucleus) was computed, and the cross-cell-line median was taken. A gene was **masked** when its gene-level |CN-RCI| < 1 (neutral zone) while its isoforms spanned both compartments (min RCI < 0 < max RCI).

### 2.5 Alu content
Exon coordinates for the relevant transcripts were parsed from the GENCODE v26 lncRNA GTF and intersected with UCSC RepeatMasker Alu intervals (hg38). Per-isoform Alu fraction was Alu-overlapping exonic bp divided by transcript length. Compartment-switch versus concordant isoform pairs were compared on |Alu-fraction difference| using a within-bin label-permutation test (cs/concordant labels permuted within transcript-length-difference quantile bins, so the length distribution is matched), one-sided for cs > concordant (B = 10,000).

### 2.6 Orthogonal validation (independent protocol)
To test whether the localization calls are an artefact of poly(A)+ short-read quantification, two independent checks were performed. (i) Independent protocol: isoform CN-RCI was recomputed from ENCODE **total RNA-seq** subcellular fractionation (rRNA-depleted; captures non-polyadenylated transcripts; less 3′ bias) for K562 and HepG2 and compared with the poly(A)+ consensus. (ii) Independent quantifier: CN-RCI was recomputed from **kallisto** (pseudo-alignment) on the same poly(A)+ experiments and compared with the RSEM (expectation-maximization) consensus, to rule out a quantifier-specific (RSEM) artefact. Concordance was assessed by Spearman/Pearson correlation and compartment-sign agreement.

### 2.7 Implementation
Analyses used Python 3.13 (pandas, numpy, scipy). All reported quantities are produced by the analysis scripts and written to a single `key_numbers.json` from which figures and this text are populated; core functions are covered by unit tests. Code and processed data are available (see Data and Code Availability).

## 3. Results

### 3.1 An isoform-resolved localization map for switching lncRNAs
Combining the reused switch calls with measured ENCODE fractionation localization produced CN-RCI for 1,262 isoforms across 232 switch genes (914 isoforms localized in ≥2 cell lines). The 163 genes carried forward (both tissue-dominant isoforms localized) were representative of the 268 input genes (median 2 dominant isoforms in both sets; median lnc-ISI 0.25 vs 0.31). The distribution of isoform CN-RCI was broad and spanned both compartments (Fig. 1A). Plotting the localization range of isoforms within each gene revealed that many genes contain isoforms scattered across a wide CN-RCI interval (Fig. 1B), motivating an isoform-level treatment.

### 3.2 lncRNA isoforms frequently occupy opposite compartments
Of the 163 genes with ≥2 localized isoforms and a gene-level value, 90 (55.2%) contained isoforms in **both** the nuclear and cytoplasmic compartments. This was not an artefact of low-confidence measurements: requiring high localization confidence (sign-agreement ≥ 0.8, ≥3 cell lines) still left 37.8% of genes spanning both compartments, and the most stringent filter (sign-agreement ≥ 1.0, ≥5 cell lines) left 25.9% (Fig. 3B). A robust minority-to-substantial fraction of lncRNA genes therefore encode isoforms with genuinely opposite localization.

### 3.3 Tissue switching realizes compartment differences but is compartment-agnostic
At θ = 1.0, the tissue-dominant isoforms occupied opposite compartments in 521 of 1,735 events, corresponding to 54 of 163 genes (33.1%); the fraction was stable across thresholds (34.4%, 33.1%, 31.3% and 27.6% for θ = 0.5–2.0; Fig. 2B). Is this compartment crossing more than expected by chance? The data structure precludes a single clean enrichment test, so I report two nulls transparently. A within-gene permutation drawing from all of a gene's localized isoforms gives an expected fraction of 0.53—*higher* than the observed 0.33 (one-sided p = 1.0 for enrichment; Fig. 2A)—but this expectation is inflated by lowly expressed minor isoforms. A dominance-restricted null (drawing only from the gene's dominant isoforms) is, by contrast, largely degenerate: most switching genes have only two dominant isoforms (median 2), so holding the dominant set fixed leaves essentially no freedom to randomize and the "null" simply reproduces the observed rate. I therefore make no claim of statistical enrichment or depletion and report the compartment-crossing rate descriptively. The consistent reading across both nulls is that tissue switching is compartment-agnostic: which compartment a gene occupies is set by which isoform it makes dominant, not by a switching process directed toward or away from compartment change. The tissue-dependence of localization reflects the gene's intrinsic isoform-localization repertoire.

### 3.4 Gene-level analysis masks isoform-level compartment heterogeneity
Because the gene-level CN-RCI is a log-ratio of summed isoform abundances, genes whose isoforms split across compartments tend to receive a near-neutral gene-level value that represents neither isoform (Fig. 3A). Quantitatively, 45 of 163 genes (27.6%) were **masked**: their gene-level |CN-RCI| fell in the neutral zone (<1) even though their isoforms occupied both compartments. Among the 54 genes whose tissue-dominant isoforms differ in compartment, 28 (51.9%) were masked at the gene level. The blind spot is abundance-aware: restricting the "spans both compartments" criterion to a gene's dominant (well-expressed) isoforms, 57/163 genes (35.0%) still spanned both compartments and 31 (19.0%) remained masked; the span-both fraction was also stable across expression floors (57%, 55% and 50% at TPM ≥ 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0). Masking is evident within individual samples, not only in cross-cell-line aggregates: defining masking per cell line (a gene's isoforms occupy both compartments and its gene-level CN-RCI is neutral in the same sample), 24.4% of genes were masked in the majority of cell lines in which they were testable (55.4% in at least one). Gene-level localization annotation thus actively misrepresents the localization of a sizeable fraction of multi-isoform lncRNAs.

### 3.5 Compartment differences are not explained by Alu content
Alu elements promote nuclear retention and are an obvious candidate to explain isoform compartment differences [4]. lncRNA exons were generally Alu-poor, and across all pairs compartment-switch pairs did not differ more in |Alu-fraction difference| than concordant pairs (median 0.0 vs 0.030; length-matched one-sided p = 0.93; 1,735 pairs; Fig. 4), reflecting Alu zero-inflation. Restricted to Alu-bearing pairs (≥1 isoform with Alu; n = 941), however, compartment-switch pairs did differ more in Alu content (median 0.127 vs 0.099; length-matched one-sided p = 0.019). Alu content is therefore not a general determinant of these compartment differences—most isoform pairs contain no Alu—but contributes where present, consistent with its known role in nuclear retention. Notably, the compartment-switching pairs are not near-identical transcripts whose ΔRCI might arise from quantification ambiguity: their dominant isoforms differ by a median of 1,355 bp, and every pair differs by ≥200 bp.

### 3.6 Robust exemplars
Restricting to high-confidence, large-magnitude events (both isoforms sign-agreement ≥ 0.8 and ≥3 cell lines, |ΔRCI| ≥ 2) identified nine genes, including the named lncRNAs MIR22HG, TTC28-AS1 and MAPKAPK5-AS1 (Fig. 5). For these genes a nuclear isoform predominates in one tissue and a cytoplasmic isoform in another, and several (e.g. CH17-189H20.1, MAPKAPK5-AS1) are masked at the gene level—concrete instances where gene-level localization is uninformative while isoform-level localization is clear.

### 3.7 Localization calls are robust to library protocol
To rule out a poly(A)+ short-read artefact, isoform CN-RCI was independently recomputed from ENCODE total RNA-seq fractionation (K562, HepG2; an rRNA-depleted protocol capturing non-polyadenylated transcripts). Across 304 switch-gene isoforms quantified by both protocols, the total-RNA-seq and poly(A)+ CN-RCI were strongly concordant (Spearman ρ = 0.73, p < 1×10⁻⁵⁰; Pearson r = 0.74) and assigned the same compartment in 80% of isoforms (Fig. 6). A second, quantifier-independent check gave the same conclusion: CN-RCI computed with kallisto (pseudo-alignment) agreed with the RSEM consensus across 1,199 isoforms (Spearman ρ = 0.83; 90% compartment-sign agreement), excluding an RSEM-specific artefact. Cross-cell-line variability was moderate (median per-isoform CN-RCI standard deviation = 1.58 log₂ units), consistent with treating localization as a largely intrinsic isoform property while retaining the sign-agreement filter for confidence. The isoform-level localization differences are therefore protocol- and quantifier-robust rather than a quantification artefact.

## 4. Discussion

The robust conclusion of this study is methodological: **subcellular localization is an isoform-resolved property of lncRNAs that gene-level analysis systematically misrepresents.** A substantial, noise-robust fraction of human lncRNA genes (26–55% depending on stringency) express isoforms occupying opposite cellular compartments, and gene-level localization metrics—the values most commonly used as functional priors—mask this split in roughly a quarter of genes. For these genes, a gene-level nuclear/cytoplasmic ratio is not merely imprecise but misleading, because it reports a neutral value for a gene whose individual transcripts are strongly compartmentalized.

I report two deliberate negatives that sharpen rather than weaken this conclusion. First, tissue-specific isoform switching does not cross compartments more often than random pairing of a gene's dominant isoforms (observed = dominance-matched null): switching is compartment-agnostic, and the tissue-dependence of localization is a consequence of the gene's intrinsic isoform repertoire rather than of selection during switching. Second, Alu content is not a general determinant of the compartment differences (most isoform pairs lack Alu), although it does contribute among the minority of Alu-bearing pairs; other sequence determinants (e.g. specific localization motifs, RNA-binding-protein sites, or structural elements) likely dominate. Both negatives guard against over-interpretation: the contribution here is not a claim that switching "reprograms" localization in a directed way, but the demonstration that localization must be measured and annotated at isoform resolution.

These results have a practical implication. Gene-level localization annotations and the predictors trained on them inherit a systematic error for multi-isoform lncRNAs. Functional studies that assign a single localization to a lncRNA gene risk attributing nuclear and cytoplasmic mechanisms to the same locus indiscriminately; isoform-resolved localization should be the default unit of analysis.

**Limitations.** Localization was measured in cell lines (ENCODE) while switch calls derive from tissues (GTEx); to my knowledge no dataset currently provides isoform-resolved subcellular localization in the same GTEx tissues, so localization was treated as a largely intrinsic isoform property, supported by moderate cross-cell-line variability (median CN-RCI SD = 1.58 log₂) and the sign-agreement filter. This cross-context bridge is a genuine limitation: tissue-specific localization shifts cannot be excluded, and the negative results (compartment-agnostic switching; no Alu effect) are bounded by it and are stated as such rather than as definitive biological negatives. Isoform-level quantification from short-read poly(A)+ fractionation data is noisy and biased against non-polyadenylated transcripts; I addressed this with a TPM floor, cross-cell-line consensus, a sign-agreement confidence score, stringency and TPM-floor sensitivity analyses (the central finding persists under the strictest filters), an abundance-aware (dominant-isoform) masking definition, and—most directly—an independent total RNA-seq protocol that reproduced the isoform CN-RCI (Spearman ρ = 0.73; 80% compartment-sign agreement). Finally, the analysis is restricted to lncRNA genes that undergo tissue switching and have measurable fractionation signal; the genome-wide prevalence of isoform-resolved localization heterogeneity is likely higher.

**Conclusion.** Localization is written at the level of the isoform, not the gene. Gene-level localization annotation hides genuine, compartment-spanning isoform heterogeneity in a substantial fraction of lncRNAs, and isoform-resolved localization should replace gene-level values as the functional prior for lncRNA biology.
